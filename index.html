In Graphite, each carbon atom is covalently bonded to three other carbon atoms

Thus, it has a giant covalent hexagonal structure in hexagonal layers,
one valence electron per carbon atom is delocalised within the structure;
these electrons are free to move to carry charges to conduct electricity
causing it to be a good conductor of electricity, and a good choice as
an anode material.

Why does this matter?

Using Silicon carbide and graphite in batteries aligns with the UNSDG goal 12
of Responsible consumption and production through reducing the need for
precious resource mining. Utilizing these carbon compounds will keep
the battery at the same efficiency as per normal but use more affordable
and abundant resources. By reducing mining activities, environmental impacts
will be reduced as well.

Using silicon carbide and graphite also aligns with the principle of the 12
Principles of Green Chemistry of “Prevention” and “Energy efficiency”.
Through the use of these carbon compounds, less energy would be required to
manufacture these batteries as less mining activities would be required,
directly correlating to the prevention of environmental destruction
and waste generated by mining activities.

These materials are crucial for Singapore's Green Plan 2030 as they pave the way
for more efficient and sustainable energy storage solutions. ⁤⁤By using batteries
with Silicon Carbide and graphite, we can store renewable energy from sources like
solar and wind power, helping to stabilize the grid and reduce our dependence on
fossil fuels. ⁤⁤These battery technologies also support the rise of transportation,
a key aspect of Singapore's efforts to cut down carbon emissions and tackle climate
change. So, SiC and graphite in batteries are in line with the objectives of the
Green Plan 2030, promoting clean energy adoption and enhancing energy efficiency
in Singapore.

As you can see...

In Silicon Carbide, one silicon atom and a carbon atom form 4 covalent bonds

Thus, it has a giant covalent tetrahedral structure, with each silicon and
carbon atom having strong intermolecular forces of attraction, thus
conducting electricity well, something that is needed in a battery.
However unlike graphite, it can be considered a semiconductor as
due to its energy levels in its valence shells, it does not conduct
electricty as well due to being unable to transfer charges as fast.

Why do we want a semiconductor?

This is because in a battery, we would like to be able to control and
limit the amount of charges in the amount of electrons flowing
through the terminal and control the current being supplied
to the compoenents which the battery is provding charge to
and avoid overloading and burning.
